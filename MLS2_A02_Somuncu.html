Written Report

Dataset Overview

DataSet1 contains 200 samples with two continuous features (x1, x2) and a binary label (y ∈ {0, 1}).
The class split is approximately 59 % positive / 41 % negative, which is mildly imbalanced but manageable.
Both features are already on [0, 1], but we still apply StandardScaler before kNN because the Euclidean
distance metric is sensitive to scale, and generalising the preprocessing pipeline is good practice.

Experiment 1 – Baseline

Setup: 70/30 train-test split, StandardScaler, k = 1, 3, 5, …, 51.

Findings:

  • The dataset is nearly linearly separable, yielding high accuracy (≥ 95%) across almost all k values.
  • k = 1 achieves the highest raw accuracy (~98.3 %) on the test set. Small k fits the training data very closely, which is rewarded here because the data is clean and
    the decision boundary is smooth.
  • As k increases beyond ~21, accuracy stabilises around 96-97 %, then dips slightly at k = 49, 51
    because very large neighbourhoods start smoothing over fine local structure.
  • Conclusion: For clean, well-separated 2D data, small odd k (1, 9, or 11) are good choices.
    A moderate value like k = 9 or 11 is preferable to k = 1 in practice, as it is less sensitive
    to individual noisy test points.

Experiment 2 – Noisy Labels (flip probability = 0.2)

Setup: Training labels flipped with p = 0.2; test labels remain clean.

Findings:

  • Overall accuracy drops by roughly 2-5 percentage points compared to the baseline.
  • The optimal k shifts upward. With label noise, very small k (e.g., k = 1) are more susceptible
    to memorising flipped labels, so the accuracy curve at low k is lower than in the baseline.
  • Larger k values perform comparatively better because averaging over more neighbours dilutes
    the effect of individual corrupted labels in the training set.
  • This is the classic bias-variance trade-off under label noise: high k introduces more bias
    but reduces the variance caused by noisy labels.
  • Conclusion: Under label noise, prefer a larger k (e.g., k = 13–21) to smooth out the noise.

Experiment 3 – Four Gaussian Noise Features

Setup: Four features drawn from N(0, 1) added to the original two features; labels remain clean.

Findings:

  • Adding irrelevant features increases the dimensionality from 2 to 6.
  • kNN uses Euclidean distance across all features, so the four noise dimensions add random
    distance components that dilute the signal from the two informative features.
  • This is the curse of dimensionality in mild form: distances become less discriminative.
  • Accuracy decreases noticeably (often by 3-8 pp at optimal k) and the accuracy-vs-k curve
    becomes flatter/more noisy.
  • The optimal k tends to increase slightly compared to the baseline, because larger
    neighbourhoods provide better averaging to compensate for the distance corruption.
  • Conclusion: Irrelevant features hurt kNN. In practice, feature selection or
    dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) should be applied before kNN.

Overall Conclusion

Scenario

Why best k changes

Clean data

Small k works well; high accuracy possible

Label noise (p=0.2)

Best k increases – larger neighbourhoods average out noise

Extra noise features

Best k increases – larger neighbourhoods compensate for diluted distances

Both perturbations shift the optimal k upward, for complementary but related reasons:
noise (whether in labels or features) increases the variance of each individual neighbour's
contribution, and a larger k reduces that variance at the cost of a small increase in bias.
