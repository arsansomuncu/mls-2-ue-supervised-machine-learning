Part A · Question 2

Why kNN Cannot Extrapolate but Linear Regression Can

A closer look at the scatter plots reveals a fundamental structural difference: the kNN predictions are entirely contained within the range of training targets, while the linear model produces values both below and above that range. This is not an artefact of a particular dataset — it is a direct consequence of how each algorithm constructs its prediction mathematically.

k-Nearest Neighbours: Prediction by Bounded Averaging

kNN prediction rule ŷ(x) = (1/k) · Σ yᵢ for the k nearest training points xᵢ

kNN predicts by computing the arithmetic mean of the k closest training targets. Because an average of a finite set of numbers always lies between the minimum and maximum of that set, the output of kNN is strictly bounded:

Boundedness guarantee min(y_train) ≤ ŷ_kNN(x) ≤ max(y_train) for all inputs x

This holds for every possible test point, including points far outside the training distribution. kNN is a purely interpolative method — it cannot extrapolate beyond the convex hull of observed targets, regardless of how extreme the input features are.

Linear Regression: Prediction by Unbounded Affine Mapping

Linear regression prediction rule ŷ(x) = w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + wₚxₚ + b = xᵀw + b

Linear regression fits a global affine function — a hyperplane — over the entire feature space. There is no mechanism that constrains the output to the range of training targets. For any feature vector x, the dot product xTw can be arbitrarily large or small depending on the magnitudes of the learned weights w and the feature values.

Concretely, if a validation or test sample has an extreme value for a feature with a large positive coefficient (e.g., very high median income), the model will predict a price above max(y_train). Conversely, a sample in a very unfavourable neighbourhood may receive a prediction below min(y_train). This is extrapolation, and it is an inherent property of any parametric model that fits an unconstrained function.

In essence: kNN memorises the target range implicitly by construction; Linear Regression learns a function that extends infinitely in all directions.

Whether extrapolation is a feature or a bug depends on the use case. For house prices capped at a known range, kNN's bound is a useful inductive bias. For forecasting scenarios where out-of-range predictions are genuinely meaningful, the linear model's ability to extrapolate is advantageous.

Moving northward in California (higher latitude) is associated with significantly lower house prices. This captures the geographic concentration of high-value real estate in the greater Los Angeles and San Francisco Bay Area coastal corridors, both of which sit at lower latitudes. Northern regions of the state tend to be less densely populated and have lower median incomes, making latitude a useful proxy for regional desirability.

3
AveOccup — Average Household Occupancy
coef ≈ −0.29 (moderate negative)

Higher average occupancy per household is a socioeconomic indicator of overcrowding. Census tracts where many people share a single dwelling unit tend to be lower-income areas with higher housing demand relative to supply. The model correctly captures this inverse relationship: as average occupancy rises, predicted house prices fall. This feature also helps the model detect outliers such as vacation properties or large multi-family buildings where price-per-unit dynamics differ.

Features such as HouseAge, AveRooms, and Population carry smaller coefficients in the scaled model, indicating a comparatively modest direct influence once income and geography are accounted for. Note that coefficient magnitude captures only linear influence; non-linear relationships may be present but invisible to this model.

Part B · Question 1

Polynomial Degree Sweep: The Bias–Variance Tradeoff in Action

A pipeline of PolynomialFeatures → StandardScaler → LinearRegression was trained for each degree d ∈ {1, 2, …, 15} on the generated 1-D noisy dataset. The plot of Training MSE versus Test MSE against d (with a logarithmic y-axis) reveals a textbook bias–variance curve, divided into three clearly distinguishable regimes.

d = 1–3
Underfitting
Both train and test MSE are high. The model is too simple to capture the degree-5 shape of the generating function. High bias dominates.
d ≈ 5–7
Sweet Spot
Train and test MSE are jointly minimised. The model complexity matches the true underlying polynomial, yielding the best generalisation.
d ≥ 8
Overfitting
Train MSE collapses toward zero while test MSE climbs steeply. The model memorises training noise and loses all ability to generalise.

Detailed Trend Description

Training MSE (blue curve): This curve decreases monotonically as the polynomial degree increases. This is mathematically guaranteed: a higher-degree model has a strictly larger hypothesis space than a lower-degree one, so it can always achieve at least as low a training error. At high degrees (d ≥ 10), the training MSE approaches zero — the model has essentially interpolated through every training point.

Test MSE (red curve): This curve follows a U-shape (best seen on the log scale). It is initially high (underfitting), decreases to a minimum near the true complexity of the generating function (degree 5), and then sharply increases again for degrees above 7–8. The divergence between the two curves at high degrees is the visual signature of overfitting.

The logarithmic scale is essential here: without it, the enormous MSE values at high degrees would compress the interesting low-MSE region into an indistinguishable flat line near zero.

Why This Happens: The Bias–Variance Tradeoff

The generating function is a degree-5 polynomial plus Gaussian noise (y = −x⁵ + 3x³ − 2x + ε, σ = 3). Models with degree d < 5 lack the representational capacity to capture the signal — they are high-bias. Models with degree d > 7 have excess parameters relative to the effective signal in 105 training samples, so they begin to fit the noise — they are high-variance.

In practical terms: every additional polynomial term adds a new coefficient that the optimiser will use to reduce training error, but once all the true signal has been captured, those extra coefficients start encoding the random noise specific to the training sample. When such a model encounters a new test point, those noise-fitted coefficients produce wildly incorrect predictions — hence the spike in test MSE.

This experiment illustrates why model complexity must always be chosen based on held-out validation error, not training error, and why the training MSE curve alone is a dangerously misleading guide for model selection.
